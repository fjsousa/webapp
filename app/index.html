About

We are enabling fire analysts to predict fire behaviour with uncertainty quantification. A technique never used in real life due to processing power constrains.

In the end of the 60’s an aerospace engineer named Richard Rothermel developed a quick way to predict wildfire behavior. He and his team at the Rocky Mountains Research Station came up with a bunch of equations and programmed a TI calculator. Then, they headed to an active wildfire to feed the model with local terrain and humidity data. After 40 years, the Rothermel model still enjoys widespread use.

Today, the models rely on satellite data, geographic information systems and real time weather forecasts. They are a precious help to fire fighters in large fires that need lots of planning and resources.

Most of these models are deterministic, which means that for a certain set of inputs you always get the same forecast. Fire analysts need to feed the models with data as accurate as possible to produce a good forecast. The problem here is that even with small wind speed errors, the prediction can have an offset of several kilometers.

One possible answer to this problem is stochastic simulations - an idea first elaborated by the fine folks at LASEF. The problem comes down to running thousands of simulations with random, realistic data. These are then post-processed to compute average fire fronts, confidence intervals and other statistics.

Each of these simulations takes little time to compute. The real challenge is to get them all done while trying to deliver a forecast before the forest becomes dust. And that’s where CrowdProcess comes in. We are spreading the several simulations among the browsers in the platform and post-processing results for you. Each forecast is presented with a 95% confidence interval, which represents best case and worst case scenarios. They are shown in orange and in red, respectively.
